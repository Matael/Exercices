\bexo


What is the best approximation in a least mean square sense by a polynomial of degree 1 of the following cloud of points  $\{x_j,y_j\}$
with 
\begin{eqnarray*}
x_1=0&y_1=1\\	
x_2=1&y_1=0\\	
x_3=2&y_1=1\\
x_4=3&y_1=2\\	
\end{eqnarray*}

\begin{center}
	\begin{tikzpicture}
	  \begin{axis}
		\addplot [only marks,blue] coordinates { (0.,1) (1,0) (2,1.) (3,2)};
				\addplot[blue] expression[domain=-1.:3.3,samples=500]{(.4)*x+0.4};
						\addplot[red]
		expression[domain=-1.:3.3,samples=500]{(.5)*x*x-1.1*x+0.9};
		\end{axis}
	\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}
 

\eexo
\solution{
concerning the polynomial of degree 1, an approximation $P(x)=\alpha x+\beta$ is sought which is associated to the minimisation of 
\begin{equation}
	\mc{C}(\alpha,\beta)=\dsum{i=1}{4} (y_i-(\alpha x_i+\beta))^2
\end{equation}
This can be rewritten in a matrical form:
\begin{equation}
	\mc{C}(\alpha,\beta)=\norm{\tb{y}-[\tb{M}]\begin{Bmatrix}
		\alpha\\  \beta
	\end{Bmatrix}}^2
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
[\tb{M}]=\left[
\begin{array}{c|c}
0 & 1\\
1 & 1\\
2 & 1\\
3 & 1\\	
\end{array}
\right]\esp \tb{y}=\begin{Bmatrix}
	1\\
	0\\
	1\\
	2
\end{Bmatrix}.
\end{equation}

The unknowns are then solution of 
\begin{equation}
	[\tb{M}]^T[\tb{M}] \begin{Bmatrix}
		\alpha\\  \beta
	\end{Bmatrix}=[\tb{M}]^T\tb{y}
\end{equation}

Hence the system is

\begin{equation}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		14 & 6\\  6 & 4
	\end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix}
		\alpha\\  \beta
	\end{Bmatrix}=
		\begin{bmatrix}
		8 \\  4
	\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
whose solution is $\alpha=\beta=0.4$. 

For the polynomial of degree $2$ ($P(x)=\alpha^2 x+\beta x+\gamma$), the matrix $[\tb{M}]$ and the linear system read:
\begin{equation}
[\tb{M}]=\left[
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
0&0 & 1\\
1&1 & 1\\
4&2 & 1\\
9&3 & 1\\	
\end{array}
\right]	\esp
	\begin{bmatrix}
		98 & 36 & 14\\  36 & 14 & 6\\
		14 & 6 & 4
	\end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix}
		\alpha\\  \beta \\ \gamma
	\end{Bmatrix}=
		\begin{bmatrix}
		22\\8 \\  4
	\end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation} 
The solution is $\alpha=0.5$, $\beta=-1.1$ and $\gamma=0.9$

}
